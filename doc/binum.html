Course description:

After taking this short course (half to one hour) you will have acquired some informal knowledge about the binary numbering system by recognizing the common ground it has with the familiar decimal numbering system.

Additionally, you will understand the meaning of a number of terms used to express computer characteristics like "megabyte" and others.

The decimal number system

According to several dictionaries, the decimal number system that we use so extensively is ...

▸ noun: a positional system of numeration that uses decimal digits and a base of 10

Notice that the definition correctly uses the indefinite article "a" accounting for the fact that there are other number systems, like for example the binary number system.

We always use the decimal system in real life in such an exclusive fashion that we might end up thinking that it is the one and only number system, but in fact it's just one in the crowd.

Like for example there is the Roman number system, that was used until recently. "Recently" is the 14th. century, when the Roman numbering system began to be replaced by the more convenient Hindu-Arabic numerals; however this process was gradual, and the use of Roman numerals in some minor applications continues to this day, for example in clock faces.

Notice that the Roman numbering system does not have a zero. The zero is said to be invented by the Indians near year 500 A.D. and is essential to any modern number system, including the decimal and the binary systems.

A decimal number system is somehow natural for we humans because we have ten fingers in our hands (mostly everybody).

Some people think that it would be better if we had twelve fingers, because this would have led to a 12-based number system, being the advantage that 12 is exactly divisible by 2, 3, 4 and 6, and that would have simplified many arithmetic problems. But we have ten fingers so we have to settle with a number base that's only divisible by 2 and 5. Families should have either two or five children, but not three.

The idea is that there is nothing special or magic in the number 10 in addition to it's fit to human anatomy.

Counting with fingers

Lets imagine a method to count with our fingers. There are smarter methods, but this one is convenient for the explanation.

We are purchasing cattle; sheep. The tally counter, like the one in the picture, has not been invented yet, so we use our fingers.

As each animal passes through a narrow aperture of the stone fence we count it by extending another finger.

After counting 10 sheep I run out of fingers but fortunately my brother is participating. He counts the number of times I run out of fingers, using his fingers the same way I do.

After counting 99 sheep both me and my brother have all our fingers commited, but fortunately the family is large and another sibling comes to the rescue counting the number of times my brother ran out of fingers and so we can count up to 999 which is enough for now.

Having the next brother count one more is the "carry" of when we learned to sum in school.

So we bought less than 1000 sheep and it was possible to count them with the participation of only three people. Notice that if we had to count more sheep, say 9999, we would need one additional brother or the litle tally counter pictured above. Each time we add a brother the maximum number increments tenfold.

The binary number system

If instead of having ten fingers we had only one, would be able to count that many sheep?

Of course the answer is yes, but with a caveat: we would need more people because each of us would run out of fingers sooner than if we had 10.

Let's start counting. As the first sheep passes I extend my single finger. When the second sheep comes I flex my finger and tell my brother B to extend his (I'm brother A). Next sheep, the third, I extend my single finger and now me and my brother B both are out of fingers so brother C joins. Another sheep comes, C extends his finger and me and B flex our's. And so on.

If a brother with his finger extended was represented with the symbol "1" and one with his finger flexed with "0", then the counting sequence would look thus:

It can also be noticed that two, the base of the binary system, expressed as a binary number is written 10, while the base of the decimal system expressed as a decimal number is also 10. This holds true for all bases.

Who would want to count in such a contrived way?

Digital devices do. Their single finger hand is a type of circuit called "flip-flop" that can be in one of two states: either "flipped" or "flopped", never an in-between state.

It is easy and safe to implement a circuit or other device type that works that way.

For example in hard disks, that store information digitally in the form of extremely tiny magnetized dots on a spinning platter, each dot represents a binary digit, a zero or a one, depending on the polarity of the magnetic field.

Another example of binary signals is a communication channels, like for example our domestic Internet connections, that transmit streams of electric signals representing bits. Millions per second, depending on the price of the connection. A "6 mega internet connection" is capable of transmitting 6 million bits per second.

Binary numbers are horrible

As we saw in the sheep counting example, binary numbers (numbers expressed in base 2) become very long quickly. Another issue is that they are made only of zeros and ones and thus they are monotonous.

It is almost impossible to remember or communicate binary numbers without making errors. For example a not-so-large number like one million expressed in binary is 11110100001001000000. How would you read this number over the phone to your rather-deaf aunt?

In decimal notation we use thousand separators to be able to read lengthy numbers more safely. For example a long number like 340923476504734876 is easier to grasp with thousand separators: 340,923,476,504,734,876 and with a bit of luck one could read it correctly.

Now, the same number in binary is this long string:

In binary there is a sort of "thousand separators" in groups of four instead of three as in decimal. Notice that four is a power of 2:  4 = 22 = 2   2  and the powers of 2 always show up when there is digital stuff around.

This is not a simple separation in fixed length groups of four bits but a "recodification".

In binary each 4-bit goup can have one of 16 possible values, and each value is assigned a symbol according to the table at the right.

The first 10 values, zero to 9, are conveniently assigned the symbols "0" through "9". Once we run out of digits we start with the letters "a" through "f".

Numbers expressed like this are called "hexadecimal numbers" because actually they are not any more in base 2 but in base 16. Hence the need for 16 symbols, the 10 digits and the first 6 letters of the alphabet.

And again, the base of the base-16 numbering system expressed as a base-16 number is 10.

byte

▸ noun: a sequence of 8 bits (enough to represent one character of alphanumeric data)

You might have been told once and again that all digital information is made out of bits.

But given how deadly simple a bit is, seems as if a lot of magic was needed between a set of bits and watching an HD film in a notebook; the film obviously is made up of bits and only bits, because bits is the only thing any computer can handle and store.

The key is organization, structure. When we were counting sheep with single fingers and we added more and more brothers to hold bits, those brothers were arranged in a certain order. Not respecting the order would have led to a count error and possibly a fight with the cattle sellers with eventual deadly consequences.

A first organization level is lumping the bits in bytes. The capacities of the computer's data storage capacities are expressed in bytes. For example as of this writing hard disks tend to have capacities like 1,099,511,627,776 bytes. Obviously we don't say such a number, we say "one terabyte" and write "1TB". This numer is another power of 2, namely 240, that is, a one followed by 40 zeros in binary notation, 10000000000 in hexadecimal.

People talk about kilobytes and megabytes. If it seems as if as soon as one learns the dimension of one of these numbers, a new one arises.

Actually it's not that "it seems", it's actually happening.

These words are measure units, like the Kg (Kilogram) that is used to measure the weight of packed sugar. A Kilogram is named so because it's made of 1,000g (a thousand grams) and "Kilo" is derived from the Greek word χίλιοι (chilioi), meaning "thousand".

Notice that the table is not about units but prefixes, hence the "-" at the end of each prefix denoting that the word is incomplete. One can only say "one mega" when it's implied what are you talking about.

The prefixes are to be combined with unit names like "meter" making, for example, "kilometer". But we are interested in computer stuff, so we stick to computer related units and how to use them.

Notice in the table that, for example, in the "mega" row 106 is related to 220. This is because 1,000,000 is close to 1,048,576 albeit not exactly equal. Actually these units should refer to the decimal, slightly lower numbers. A new set of units is out there that refers to the 2's power numbers, like "mibi" that means exactly 1,048,576-whatever. The prefixes are "kibi", "mebi", "gibi", "tebi" and so on, seldom seen.

Bytes

The first PCs had memory ("RAM" or "main memory") sizes of about 32 Kilobytes or 64 Kilobytes (KB). Current computers have 2 gigabytes and more, about 100,000 times growth.

When you purchase a new PC you want it to have as much RAM as possible, but over time it will not be enough and you will want to expand it by adding current versions of the memory modules shown in the picture (512MB each, year 2006).

So memory size was measured in KB (kilobytes), MB (megabytes), and is expressed GB (gigabytes) for now.

Hard disks, used by the computer to store your files, had capacities measured in MB , then GB and currently TB.

Both main memory and disk sizes are expressed as an ever-growing number of bytes.

Hz

One "Hertz" is one cycle per second. It's a frequency unit. For example alternate current (the "AC" in "AC-DC") oscilates at 50Hz or 60Hz depending on the country. Sounds range from 20Hz bass to 20KHz (20,000Hz) high pitch.

The speed of PCs is measured as the number of "cycles" per second they can perform, the number of Hz.

The first PC micro processors ran at 8,66MHz, current PCs have speeds in the order of 2 to 3 GHz, 1,000 times faster. A few years ago the race for speed hit phisycal limits, so the microprocessor makers started to lump two or four processors in a single unit so the theoretical computing power is the number of GHz times the number of processors in the chip.

To do their work the microprocessors need to fetch data from and store data into, the main memory. The memory speed needs to be in tune with the processor's speed else there might be a significant impact in overall throughput. That's why at some point in time the manufacturers started to advertise the memory speed - also measured in MHz, or GHz, in addition to the processor speed.

bit/sec

Internet connection speeds are measured in bits per second. Bits, not bytes.

Old telephone modems, used for dial-up connections to the Internet in the nineties, had speeds ranging from 14,4Kb/s (notice the lowercase "b" meaning "bit", not "Byte").

Current broadband connections are much faster, in the order of 10Mb/s or Mbps.

That's why when you are downloading big files the number of bytes per second reported by the web browser is at best about one tenth of your nominal speed.

The dictionary definition for "byte" given above is somehow wrong (grossly outdated) in the part that reads "enough to represent one character". A byte, made of 8 bits, can hold exactly 28 or 256 different values. This is not enough for the asian alphabets made of thousands of different characters, neither for the myriad of occidental characters and symbols needed, check the "CopyPasteCharacter" page to see for yourself.

A byte was enough to represent the limited set of characters that was workable until computers turned really international. Now the Unicode Character Standard defined more than 95,000 characters with 2 and 4 byte per character codes.

The table shows a relationship between byte values and displayed or printed characters. This arrangement is one of the many possible "encodings". This particular encoding is vastly used in a gazillion web pages, millions of people abide by it, is known as "ISO 8859/1" or "Latin 1" and is an extension of the older ASCII encoding. This is not Unicode, which is too complex for the purpose of this course.

To interpret the table remember that a byte has 8 bits and thus it can be expressed as 2 hexadecimal digits. The table contains 256 cells (16   16) and maps from byte values to character symbols, for example the uppercase "W" is in the row with "5-" at the left (first hex character) and in the column with "-7" at the top (second hex character) - try parking the mouse pointer over the "W" in the table. This means that the hexadecimal code for the "W" is 57, or 0101 0111, the binary equivalents of the hex digits 5 and 7.

So this is how it works at its lowest level: a massive agreement on how to interpret a bit structure, in this case a simple set of 8 bits. Millions of computers displaying a "W" when there is a byte containing 01010111.

Bytes and colors

This section is another example of structure. You have heard of specifications for monitor screens that mention "24 bit color" or "16 million colors", or the acronym "RGB", or "screen resolution". This section is about it.

In computer graphical output, like a screen or printed matter, the text and the images are made out of "pixels", tiny dots emitting light of a given color. If you'd look at the screen with a magnifying glass you would notice them. The pixels tidily aligned in rows and columns of an array.

As technology progresses the pixels tend to become smaller and smaller. A rather recent breakthrough (as of this writing, Jan 2014) are the "Retina" class displays that exhibit the highest pixel densities at 200~300+ pixels per inch (ppi), about twice as before.

With the pixel sizes shrinking, the number of pixels needed to put together a screen of a given size has to grow. Doubling the ppi means multiplying the number of pixels by 4, right? Because it doubled both the vertical and the horizontal sizes.

For example the first two generations of Apple iPads have screens displaying 1024 768 pixels, and the following 3rd and 4th generations have 2048 1536 resolutions (786,432 vs. 3,145,728 pixels), all of them with 9.7 inch screens.

Similarly, we have also witnessed the grow of the advertised number of pixels in digital photo cameras, from a few hundred thousands up to many millions.

Each pixel's color is encoded (most of the times) using 3 bytes, one for each of the three primary colors: Red, Green and Blue, thus the "RGB" acronym. The pixel behaves as three tiny lights tightly packed together: a red light, a green light and a blue one. Each byte controls the amount of light of each color that the pixel has to emit to render the desired color.

Each of the three bytes of a pixel's color is treated as a number between 0 and 255 (binary 00000000 all the way through 11111111) meaning the amount of the associated color to be emitted by the pixel. If the three bytes contain zero the pixel will not emit any light of any color and the result will be black. On the other side, if all three contain the value 255 the pixel will fully emit all three colors and the outcome will be white. The arrangement (0, 0, 255) means no red, no green and full blue, so the pixel will render primary blue.

Binary numbers

We know that there are 32-bit computers and 64-bit computers. Previously there were 8-bit computers and 16-bit computers. What does this mean? Let's get into it.

As we have already seen, in a byte we can store a number with a very limited set of values: only from zero to 255.

Lumping two bytes together and managing the two bytes "word" as a single value expands the range of possible values to 216 = 65536 different values.

For many applications these values are considered non-negative values ranging from zero though 65535. Other applications need to operate with negative numbers and in this case half of the values are zero or positive, and the other half are negative. The first bit of the first byte is designated to contain the sign, a "1" for negative numbers and a "0" for the others. Negative numbers are stored in a weird format called "two's complement".

An application type that uses a lot of unsigned binary integers is "address arithmetic.

Inside the computer the memory ("RAM" for Random Acces Memory) can be imagined (simplyfying) as a long array of bytes, each one having an address. The first one has address zero, the next has 1, and so on. Nowadays home PCs have 2GB bytes and more. That's about 2000 million bytes.

When a program is operating, it has its data in the computer's memory (BTW, the running program is also in the same memory). If the program implemented a social-media application, and at a given moment it had a contacts list loaded in memory, and each contact was 120 bytes long (enough bytes to store the contact's data as the number, name, and something else), and all contact's data was stored in consecutive memory "positions" then to access the 12th. contact the program would have to calculate its address, thus:

    address of first contact + 120   (contact number − 1)

This is address arithmetic and it happens a lot of times. It is done with unsigned binary integer numbers because the byte addresses are positive, and because binary arithmetic is so fast.

Normal computers can't directly add or multiply two numbers stored in memory. The programs have to use "registers", thus (for the example just above):

  • load "contact number" in register
  • decrement register by one
  • multiply register by 120
  • add "address of first contact" to register

and the desired result would be in the register ready to be used.

Think of the register as something similar to the pocket calculator display, that witnesses all the calculations you do.

Similarly, pocket calculators have numeric displays measured in terms of number of digits, and the computers are classified in terms of the size of their registers expressed as a number of bits. Thus the 16-bit, 32-bit and 64-bit PCs.

Decades ago the computer operational circuits were very expensive. A 32-bit CPU's circuits are more complex that a 16-bit's so the 32-bit CPU was much more expensive. It's not only the registers that double their sizes, all the computer's internal data paths have to be enlarged too to fit the new "word" width (a "word" is the data that a CPU can handle in a single swoop or "cycle" and is measured by its number of bits).

Enlarging the computer's word size is a way to make it faster, by enabling it to operate on twice as much data in the same time: a computer that has to use an 8-byte data item can retrieve it from its memory and store it in a register in a single cycle if it's a 64-bit CPU (because 8 bytes span 64 bits), but it will need at least two cycles if it was a 32-bit one. Think of the internal data paths as 32-lanes highways or 64-lanes super-highways. Both might have the same speed limit, but the wider one will be able to handle twice as much traffic per hour.

Operation of the UI

Smart scroll: This is a feature aimed at preventing eye strain.
Once you have read about half of the page, press the space bar: the text will scroll gently and reposition at the top of a paragraph or whatever.
If there is a header, the header will be set atop the top of the window, no split test lines.
Pressing shift + spacebar repositions the content back, in smaller chunks (single paragraph).

There are a few features showcased, from dozens that will come.

Warning: please close each feature before opening another, the appropriate logic is not yet implemented.

Progress indicator: The top-left element is intended to show the reader the current position withing the lengthy text.

The numbers are the number of pages already read (23) and not-yet-read(77) . The gray bar represents the current reading position, that will move down as the reading progresses: it is like those bookmarks inserted between a paper book's pages.

Of course, reopening the page, may be in another device, will take the reader to the exact previous position.

Menu: This icon slides a "control panel" over the reading area, that in the future will have useful data for the reader, and configuration options. Clicking again the icon or the panel hides it.

Table of content Clicking this icon makes the TOC visible, clicking again hides it.

The TOC contains the article's headings. Clicking one takes the reader to the corresponding reading position and hides the TOC.

The TOC is organized as a hierarchy of headers and sub-headers. Clicking in the [-] and [+] icons conceals or reveals a branch of said hierarchy.

Highlighting: The reader can highlight text by selecting it and clicking the yellow button.

Currently the highlights are not persisted, as will be in the real application.

Help: This icon displays this that you are reading now (thanks!). Clicking again hides this text.

Purpose and description of the application

You are a reader (or, as an evaluator, you are impersonating one)

You, as a user, are a person who reads on screen. Not just a glimpse at the news, or a facebook posting: you really read, you are a reader.

Historically the web has been providing more and more content, but not much has been done in order to help readers to overcome many of the problems that stem from reading on screen content. Albeit sites are much better than years ago, and also screens, published pages haven't changed much.

What do the editors do?

Those who publish, the editors, are focused on providing you the best content thay can. At the same time, they remain oblivious of what can they do in order to relief their beloved readers from that eye strain or neck ache.

The purpose of this application is to enhance the online published pages in order to make the on-screen reading experience better.

The method is twofold. It is about helping to control strain, and also providing additional useful features not available on paper.

The editors will be interested in implementing this because it comes with a lot of back-end features like detailed tracking of the usage of the texts, useful for the authors to realize what parts are read, where the readers quit, and more.

What does this application do?

In order to manage the eye strain produced by on-screen reading, it has a new soft scroll functionality that slides the text in a natural way, slowly, unlike the normal instant page jums that come with the browsers. This allows the reader to be more confident that the text will remain there allowing them to blink more frequently (eye strain comes, in part, from not blinking enough which produces dry eyes).

Some paper features available

Highlighting: On paper the readers can highlight the most interesting parts of the text, to be able to review it quickly. This UI has an easy to use highlighting feature: the reader highlights the text with the mouse (and later by touching it) and clicks the yellow highlight button.

In the finished app the highlights are persisted in the backend, in this prototype one can highlight text fragments but they are not persisted, not even locally.

Notes: It will be possible to take notes that will be persisted in the backend.
